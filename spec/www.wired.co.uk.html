Apple's Jonathan Ive on being brave enough to say 'no'

By Olivia Solon
30 July 12
Apple

Maker of the iPhone, Mac, iPad, iPod and birthplace of everything iTunes and iCloud

Apple has been preparing for mass production on a number of occasions and has been forced to pull the product because it's not good enough, according to Jonathan Ive, speaking at the British Embassy's Creative Summit.

The company's senior vice president of industrial design explained: "You realise you are talking a little bit too loud about the virtues of something, because you are trying to convince yourself that it's good. You have this horrible feeling deep down in your tummy that you know it's OK, but not great. Some of the bravest things we have done is stop at that moment and say 'this is good, but not great'."

He said that Apple doesn't get credit for saying no a lot because "by definition" people aren't aware of it. He added that Apple has never said no to save money. "When we were going out of business, we didn't close down divisions to save money, we said no so we had the capacity and ability to focus on what we thought was important," he said.

He felt that one of the hardest things was saying no without the benefit of hindsight. "We have to say no in the absence of a better idea. If we are honest about wanting to make the best possible products, that means saying no because we don't think it's good enough."

The company also has to hold products back even when they think they are really compelling because the team doesn't think it can solve a fundamental problem. He said when he used an early prototype of the iPhone he would put the phone to his ear and it would dial a number. In order to get over this problem, the iPhone had to have sensors to detect ear shapes and skin tones so that the touch was deactivated when the phone was placed near the ear.

"This is one example where we really thought it wasn't going to work. If you are truly innovating, you don't have a prototype you can refer to. You can't be assured that there is a solution. If we can't solve fundamental problems, we've had to put products aside."

Ive criticised people who said that Apple products are so "inevitable" or intuitive that they couldn't be any other way. "There are many ways it could be, but one of our goals it to try and make it appear inevitable."

He related what he described as one of his most profound memories from his youth -- when he bought his first Mac after years of unsatisfactory experiences with computers. "I did as many other people do which was convince myself that it was my problem, when often it's because we are forced to use horrible products. I discovered a Mac and it was wonderful and I realised I wasn't totally inept."

He then realised something "a little sobering". He said that he had a very clear sense from the object about the people who had made it, what their values were and what preoccupied them. "This was terrifying as a design student. I realised that what I make will point back at me."

However, it made him want to find out more about "this group of people in California". "Why did they get together and what drove them to make this product?"
